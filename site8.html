Walker case

Fort Ontario was significant in the Civil War as a recruitment center for soldiers and Oswego County sent a disproportionately high number of recruits to the Union army. Dr. Mary Walker was only associated with the Fort in her later years. The 1906 photograph of Dr. Walker standing under an umbrella was taken on the road in front of Officers Row, a line of brick buildings no longer there, which was near the current road between the main and overlook parking lots. The occasion was the July 4th Independence Day Parade which also celebrated the reopening of Fort Ontario after a huge construction and modernization project. In the background is the main ballfield and houses along East 9th Street. She was later treated at the Fort Ontario Army Hospital #5 in the summer of 1918 after she suffered from a "stroke of paralysis". The Fort Ontario newspaper, the Ontario Chronicles, reported her death in February 1919, saying, "The career of the woman physician, one of the first in the history of the country, was picturesque and brimful. She was the first woman to hold a commission as assistant army surgeon, having served in that capacity in the Civil War. Dr. Walker considered it the greatest personal compliment to be mistaken for a man. Her death will be gravely felt within the ranks of feminism, a cause which she did much to further through her long life."
